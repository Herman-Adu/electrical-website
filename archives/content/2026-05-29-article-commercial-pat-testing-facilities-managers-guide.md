## What PAT Means at Portfolio Scale

PAT testing in a commercial setting is not the same problem as booking an annual inspection visit for a small office. A facilities manager responsible for multiple buildings, hundreds of portable appliances, or a logistics operation with high-turnover equipment is managing a compliance programme — with its own scheduling logic, documentation requirements, and insurance implications.

The regulatory basis is the Electricity at Work Regulations 1989, which require that electrical systems and equipment be maintained in a safe condition. The Regulations impose a duty; they do not specify how to fulfil it. The HSE's recognised method is the IET Code of Practice for In-Service Inspection and Testing of Electrical Equipment (4th edition). Annual testing is not mandated for all equipment — it is the correct frequency for some categories and excessive for others.

This guide is for facilities managers responsible for PAT compliance across commercial portfolios: office buildings, warehouses, retail premises, or mixed-use sites. It covers how the IET CoP frequency framework applies, what insurance conditions typically require, how to structure documentation, and the specific considerations for logistics and high-turnover environments.

## Frequency, Risk, and Insurance

**Equipment class and environment**

The IET Code of Practice classifies portable appliances by equipment class and operating environment. Class I equipment depends on an earth connection for protection — desktop computers, floor-standing printers, catering equipment, power tools. Class II equipment is double-insulated — most power supplies, some portable tools, battery chargers. Class III operates at safety extra-low voltage (SELV) — below 50V AC.

For each class, the IET CoP provides recommended retesting intervals by environment. Class I equipment in a standard office — a desktop computer used eight hours per day — might reasonably be on a four-year retest cycle based on risk assessment. The same class of equipment in a commercial kitchen or warehouse, subject to physical handling and moisture, warrants an annual or sub-annual interval.

**Insurance conditions**

Many commercial and property owner insurance policies contain conditions around electrical equipment maintenance. If an electrical fault causes a fire, business interruption, or other loss, and the insurer's investigation reveals that equipment was not maintained to an industry-recognised standard, the policy conditions may not be met — resulting in a reduced or declined claim. The IET Code of Practice is the recognised standard. What your policy specifically requires — annual testing, IET CoP compliance, or something else — should be confirmed with your insurance broker in writing and held on file.

**Logistics and high-turnover environments**

A logistics or warehousing environment presents PAT challenges a standard office programme does not. Equipment changes hands between shifts, is moved between buildings, and is sometimes replaced ad hoc with non-standard items. In a distribution centre with continuous shift patterns, the risk is not just that equipment ages between test dates — it is that the equipment in active use may not be the equipment that was tested. Managing this requires operational controls alongside testing: visible pass labels, a defined incoming equipment quarantine process, and a register updated in real time.

**Asset register requirements**

An asset register is the operational foundation of any commercial PAT programme. Without one, retesting intervals cannot be managed, failed equipment cannot be tracked through remediation, and documentation cannot be produced on request. The minimum a commercial register needs: unique asset identifier, equipment description and class, test date, next test date, test result (insulation resistance in MΩ, earth continuity in Ω for Class I), remedial action taken, and the contractor's name. A barcode or QR system linked to a management database makes scheduling systematic — the only practical approach for large estates.

**Contractor documentation**

The contractor delivering your programme determines the quality of the documentation you receive. A contractor who issues pass/fail labels and a one-line summary has delivered a testing visit, not a compliance programme. The documentation you need is a test register that identifies each appliance individually, records its class, shows numeric test results, notes visual failures separately from electrical failures, and includes the tester's identification and qualification — in a format you can retain and access without contacting the contractor.

## Structuring Your PAT Programme

Establish the asset register before the testing programme, not alongside it. If a register does not exist, commission the PAT contractor to carry out an inventory audit as part of the first testing cycle — walk the estate, tag all in-scope equipment, test it, and produce the baseline register in a single programme. Starting with a known, tagged inventory eliminates uncertainty about what is in scope.

Apply the IET Code of Practice risk matrix to determine frequency bands for each equipment category. Group your estate by environment and within each environment by equipment class. The IET CoP tables give recommended initial intervals — apply them as the starting point for your written risk assessment. The written assessment is what demonstrates the frequency decision was made on identifiable grounds.

Confirm your insurance policy's electrical maintenance conditions with your broker before setting your schedule. Some policies specify annual testing regardless of risk category; others reference the IET CoP and defer to the duty holder's assessment. Hold your broker's written confirmation alongside your PAT register.

Implement an incoming equipment control process. Any equipment arriving on site — purchased, transferred, borrowed, or hired — should be PAT tested before entering service or quarantined until tested. A simple tagging system — green for tested, yellow for quarantine pending test — is enough to make the control visible to operational staff.

Commission the contractor to update your asset register, not just issue test labels. The test visit should conclude with an updated electronic register, exported in a format you can access and back up independently. The register's value is in its accessibility when it is needed.

Review your frequency bands annually. Equipment categories change as operations evolve — new items enter service, environments change with tenancy. A brief annual review keeps the programme current and the written justification accurate.

## Key Takeaways

- PAT testing frequency is determined by risk assessment, not mandatory annual schedules — the IET Code of Practice provides the recognised framework; document the assessment, not just the testing
- Commercial insurance policies often contain electrical maintenance conditions — confirm what yours requires in writing from your broker before setting your programme
- Class I equipment in kitchens, warehouses, and construction environments warrants significantly shorter retesting intervals than equivalent equipment in office environments
- An asset register with individual item tracking, numeric test results, and remedial action records is the compliance foundation — a pass/fail label is an operational control, not a compliance document
- Incoming equipment controls are as important as the testing schedule — untested equipment entering service is the most common source of compliance gaps in high-turnover environments
- The contractor delivers the documentation, not just the test — insist on a test register producing numeric results, identifying equipment class, and available in a format you can retain independently

## Documentation That Works

Compliance with the Electricity at Work Regulations in a commercial setting does not require a rigid annual testing schedule applied uniformly to every portable appliance. It requires demonstrable maintenance of electrical equipment to a safe standard, with a documented risk assessment justifying frequency decisions and test records that can be produced on request.

The test register is the document that matters most in a compliance challenge. It demonstrates that the programme was systematic rather than reactive, that equipment failures were identified and addressed, and that the duty holder took the Electricity at Work Regulations seriously as an operational obligation. A stack of test labels confirms a visit took place. A properly maintained register confirms a programme is in place.

For facilities managers inheriting a PAT programme without clear documentation, the practical step is a baseline audit — a single visit by the PAT contractor to inventory, tag, test, and register all in-scope equipment across the estate. That baseline produces the asset register and sets the frequency schedule. From that point, the programme is systematic: scheduled retests, incoming equipment control, and annual risk assessment review.
